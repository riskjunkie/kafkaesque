Department I

The Charge

Statement of the Matter

You already know how this goes.

You bought something on a premise you couldn't fully explain, to yourself or anyone else. The chart moved on information you were never issued. Support vanished the exact moment you needed it, and the people who'd set the terms were never named, never elected, and never in the room to answer for it. When you asked what happened, the answer always had the same shape: technically, nothing went wrong. And also, everything did.

That feeling has a name older than the industry currently taking credit for it. A century before token launches existed, Kafka described a system that judges you by rules it declines to publish, administered by an authority that never appears in person to explain itself. Swap the court for the whales, the law for the roadmap — the fit stops being a metaphor.

Most projects either pretend this isn't true, or turn it into the whole joke and stop there. We're doing the less comfortable thing: naming it on the record, and then building past it — with structure, not slogans.

That starts with opening the file. Dev wallet holds 5% — public, labeled, on-chain — and it burns on the way up: 1% at $100K, 1% at $500K, 1% at $1M, 1% at $5M, and the last 1% at $10M. By the time this thing is established, the dev bag is zero. Mint authority renounced. Liquidity burned — not locked, burned, permanently, verifiable on-chain. No pre-sale, no private allocation, no OTC handshake. Launched on the same bonding curve as everyone else — no back doors, no head starts. The system that judged you in the dark operates in daylight here.

Then: revenue that isn't printed from nothing. Half of all creator fees go to The Castle's weekly winner — paid in USDC, not $KAFKA, so the winner never becomes sell pressure. The other half goes to treasury for buybacks and burns. The supply shrinks. That's not a promise about price — it's math. Only $KAFKA holders can compete. What happens next is still on you, same as always.
